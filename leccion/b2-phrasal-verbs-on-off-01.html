Phrasal Verbs with ON vs OFF

B2 — vocabulary, speaking, listening, reading

Sofía: Did you turn off the stove before we left?

Ben: I think so. Or maybe I turned it on. It's hard to remember.

Kenji: We should probably go back and check, then.

Sofía: We can't keep putting off important checks like this, Ben.

Common mistake: “Please close the lights when you leave.” — Correct: “Please turn off the lights when you leave.”

'On' and 'off' are opposites in phrasal verbs too — turning something on/off, calling a plan on/off, or depending on someone all use this same pair of particles.

Practice

  1. Sofía ___ the lights before anyone arrived. (turned on, turned off, called off)
  2. They didn't ___ the meeting, just postponed it. (call off, turn on, carry on)
  3. You can always ___ Abuela Carmen for good advice. (count on, turn off, call off)
  4. 'Turn off the lights' is more natural in English than 'close the lights'.
  5. 'Call off' means to continue with a plan.
  6. 'Put off' means to postpone something.
  7. Ben ___ (turn, past) off his phone to focus.
  8. They decided to ___ on with the project.
  9. Can I ___ on you tomorrow?
  10. Unscramble: please, turn, off, the, lights, when, you, leave
  11. Unscramble: they, didn't, call, off, the, meeting
  12. Find the mistake: Please close the lights when you leave.
  13. Sofía activated the lights. — Rewrite using 'turn on'.
  14. They cancelled the event. — Rewrite using 'call off'.
  15. Can I count on you tomorrow? — Answer with a short answer (Yes).
  16. Ben turned on his phone. — Write the opposite action.
  17. Do you always turn off your phone at night? (short answer)

Listening

Sofía: Did you turn off the stove before we left?

Ben: I think so. Or maybe I turned it on.

Kenji: We should go back and check.

Sofía: We can't keep putting off important checks like this.

Ben: Fine, let's go back. We can always count on my memory being terrible.

Reading

Every trip with Ben includes at least one moment of doubt: did he turn off the stove, the iron, the lights? Sofía has learned to count on this happening and always double-checks before they leave. Once, they had to call off an entire dinner reservation just to drive back and confirm the stove really was off (it was). Kenji finds the whole ritual entertaining — in Japan, he says, nobody would dream of putting off a safety check the way Ben does. But somehow, nothing has ever actually gone wrong, and Sofía suspects that's exactly why Ben keeps carrying on the same way.

  • Sofía always double-checks before they leave.
  • They once called off a dinner reservation to check the stove.
  • The stove was actually left on that time.
  • Kenji finds Ben's habit completely normal in Japan.
