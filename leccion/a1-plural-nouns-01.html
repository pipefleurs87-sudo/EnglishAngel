Plural Nouns — regular & irregular

A1 — grammar, vocabulary, listening, reading, writing

Sofía: Moving day! There are ten boxes: five with books, five with... more books.

Ben: My feet hurt. And I dropped a box on my teeth. Don't ask.

Kenji: In Japanese, one book and ten books are the same word. English has rules... and then it has children, men and women.

Abuela Carmen: And people, my dear. One person, two people. English is like my recipes: rules first, exceptions forever.

Common mistake: “I have two childs.” — Correct: “I have two children.”

Plurals say there is more than one — most nouns add -s or -es, but the most common words in English break the rules: children, people, feet.

Practice

  1. The plural of 'child' is ___. (children, childs, childrens)
  2. The plural of 'box' is ___. (boxs, boxes, boxies)
  3. One person, two ___. (persons, peoples, people)
  4. 'City' becomes 'cities' because it ends in consonant + y.
  5. The plural of 'foot' is 'foots'.
  6. Colombia has many beautiful ___ (city).
  7. My ___ (foot) are tired after the walk.
  8. There are three ___ (watch) in the box.
  9. Unscramble: the, children, are, in, the, garden
  10. Unscramble: are, there, many, people, at, the, market
  11. Find the mistake: I have two childs.
  12. Find the mistake: There are many persons at the party.
  13. Find the mistake: My tooths are white.

  15. The children are at school. — Write the yes/no question.
  16. Are the boxes heavy? — Answer with a short answer (Yes).
  17. The children are at school today. — Write the negative form.
  18. There aren't any watches in the box. — Write the affirmative form (use 'some').

Listening

Sofía: OK, moving plan. How many boxes are there?

Ben: Ten boxes. Five are full of books.

Kenji: And the other five?

Ben: More books. And two watches, three caps... and a fish.

Sofía: A fish?!

Ben: Don't worry! Fish don't need many things. He has his box... with water, obviously.

Reading

Market day in the neighborhood. There are people everywhere: men with boxes of mangoes, women with baskets, children running between the tables. Abuela Carmen knows everybody. 'Those two men sell the best fish,' she says. 'And that woman has cherries from her own trees.' Kenji counts in his notebook: fifteen families, six dogs, hundreds of apples, and one grandmother who knows the names of all the people — and the prices of all the things.

  • There are many people at the market.
  • The two men sell cherries.
  • Kenji counts fifteen families.
  • Abuela Carmen doesn't know anybody at the market.
