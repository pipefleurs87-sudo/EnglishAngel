Idioms — Money & Business (Abuela Carmen's Wisdom)

B2 — vocabulary, reading, speaking, listening

Sofía: This new laptop cost me an arm and a leg.

Abuela Carmen: Then don't cut corners on the case — protect it, or you'll pay through the nose fixing it later.

Sofía: You always have a saying for everything.

Abuela Carmen: When you sold mangoes for thirty years, you learn every trick to make ends meet.

Common mistake: “The phone cost an arm and a leg, so now I only have one arm.” — Correct: “The phone cost an arm and a leg, so I can't afford anything else this month.”

Idioms about money describe cost, risk and survival using images (arms, legs, corners, noses) that have nothing literally to do with money — the meaning is entirely figurative.

Practice

  1. That new phone ___ Ben an arm and a leg. (cost, paid, made)
  2. Abuela Carmen says cutting corners always ___ more later. (costs, pays, goes)
  3. They ___ through the nose for that apartment. (paid, cost, cut)
  4. 'Cost an arm and a leg' means something is very cheap.
  5. 'Cut corners' means to do something poorly to save time or money.
  6. 'Go bust' means a business becomes very successful.
  7. Sofía doesn't ___ corners, even in a hurry.
  8. The business didn't go ___, despite the hard year.
  9. We didn't ___ ends meet that first year.
  10. Unscramble: that, new, phone, cost, Ben, an, arm, and, a, leg
  11. Unscramble: they, paid, through, the, nose, for, that, apartment
  12. Find the mistake: The phone cost an arm and a leg, so now I only have one arm.
  13. The laptop was extremely expensive. — Rewrite using 'cost an arm and a leg'.
  14. The company failed financially. — Rewrite using 'go bust' or 'go under'.
  15. Is it a safe bet to invest in her studio? — Answer with a short answer (Yes).
  16. Sofía cuts corners on every project. — Write the negative form.
  17. Have you ever paid through the nose for something? (short answer)

Listening

Sofía: This new laptop cost me an arm and a leg.

Abuela Carmen: Then don't cut corners on the case — protect it, or you'll pay through the nose fixing it later.

Sofía: You always have a saying for everything.

Abuela Carmen: When you sold mangoes for thirty years, you learn every trick to make ends meet.

Sofía: Did you ever worry the stand would go bust?

Abuela Carmen: Every single week. And it never did.

Reading

Abuela Carmen sold mangoes on the same corner for thirty years, and she says every idiom about money in English has a mango-stand equivalent in her own history. When Sofía complains that a new laptop cost her an arm and a leg, Abuela Carmen nods — she remembers years when a single bad harvest meant paying through the nose for fruit she used to grow herself. When people ask if her business ever nearly went bust, she just laughs: 'Every week felt like it might. And every week, somehow, we made ends meet.' Her one rule, repeated to Sofía more times than either can count: never cut corners on the thing your reputation depends on.

  • Abuela Carmen sold mangoes for thirty years.
  • She says her business never felt like it might fail.
  • Her business actually went bust at some point.
  • Her rule is to never cut corners on her reputation.
