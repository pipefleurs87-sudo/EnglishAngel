Ellipsis & Substitution

C1 — grammar, speaking, writing

Kenji: Sofía can speak French, and so can I, actually.

Ben: Really? I had no idea. Will it rain later?

Sofía: I hope not, we have plans outside.

Abuela Carmen: I think so, actually. Bring an umbrella. I always do.

Common mistake: “Sofía can speak French, and Kenji can too so.” — Correct: “Sofía can speak French, and so can Kenji.”

Ellipsis and substitution let you avoid repeating a whole verb phrase — 'so can I', 'I think so', 'neither does she' — by replacing it with a short, economical structure.

Ellipsis & substitution shortcuts
PatternExample
so + auxiliary + subjectSofía can speak French, and so can Kenji.
neither/nor + auxiliary + subjectSofía doesn't like meetings, and neither does Ben.
verb + so / not (belief, opinion)Will it rain? I hope not. / I think so.
  • Sofía can speak French, and so can Kenji.
  • I think so.
  • Ben wanted to leave early, and so did Kenji.
  • She finished her coffee and then did the same with mine.
  • I hope not.
  • Sofía doesn't like early meetings, and neither does Ben.
  • I don't think so.
  • Do you think it will rain? I hope not.
  • Can Sofía speak French? I think so.
  • Did Ben want to leave early? I believe so.
  • Will it rain tomorrow? I hope not.
  • Can Kenji speak French too? Yes, so can he.
  • Did Ben want to leave? Yes, and so did Kenji.

Practice

  1. Sofía can speak French, and ___ Kenji. (so can, can so, so does)
  2. Will it rain? I hope ___. (not, no, don't)
  3. Sofía doesn't like early meetings, and ___ Ben. (neither does, so does, neither do)
  4. 'So can I' repeats the full verb phrase instead of shortening it.
  5. 'I think so' is a short way of avoiding repeating a whole clause.
  6. 'Neither does she' is used to agree with a negative statement.
  7. Ben wanted to leave early, and ___ Kenji.
  8. I don't think ___.
  9. Can Sofía speak French? I think ___.
  10. Unscramble: Sofía, can, speak, French, and, so, can, Kenji
  11. Unscramble: Sofía, doesn't, like, early, meetings, and, neither, does, Ben
  12. Find the mistake: Sofía can speak French, and Kenji can too so.
  13. Sofía can speak French. Kenji can speak French too. — Combine using 'so can'.
  14. Sofía doesn't like early meetings. Ben doesn't like them either. — Combine using 'neither does'.
  15. Will it rain tomorrow? — Answer briefly expressing hope that it won't.
  16. I think so. — Write the negative form (you don't believe it).
  17. Do you think English gets easier at C1? Answer briefly.

Listening

Kenji: Sofía can speak French, and so can I, actually.

Ben: Really? I had no idea. Will it rain later?

Sofía: I hope not, we have plans outside.

Abuela Carmen: I think so, actually. Bring an umbrella.

Ben: Sofía doesn't trust the forecast, and neither do I anymore.

Kenji: Neither does anyone in this group, honestly.

Reading

Nobody in the group fully trusts the weather forecast anymore, and neither, frankly, does Abuela Carmen — though she's usually right anyway. 'Will it rain today?' Kenji asked before their walk. 'I think so,' she said, already reaching for her umbrella. Sofía hoped not; she had plans that only worked if it stayed dry. It didn't. Ben, who'd left his umbrella at home again, got soaked — and so, predictably, did his phone, safely tucked in his pocket as always. 'I told you so,' Abuela Carmen didn't say, mostly because she didn't need to.

  • Abuela Carmen trusts the forecast completely.
  • Sofía hoped it would rain.
  • Ben's phone got wet again.
  • Abuela Carmen said 'I told you so' out loud.
