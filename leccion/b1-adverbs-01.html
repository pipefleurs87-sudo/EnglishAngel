Adverbs of Manner — quickly, carefully & comparatives

B1 — grammar, vocabulary, listening, reading, writing

Sofía: I work quickly, but I check everything carefully. Two adverbs, one designer.

Ben: I sing loudly. The neighbors say I sing badly. Both things can be true.

Kenji: And you can say it two ways: 'I quickly learned salsa' or 'I learned salsa quickly'. Before the verb, or at the end!

Abuela Carmen: And I dance better than all of you. The most gracefully, in fact. It was proven in 1975.

Common mistake: “She sings beautiful.” — Correct: “She sings beautifully.”

Adverbs of manner say HOW something is done — most end in -ly, and they can go before the verb or at the end of the sentence.

adjective → adverb (-ly)
RuleFormExample
Formationadjective + -lyquick → quickly · careful → carefully
Spelling: -y → -ilyeasy → easily · happy → happilyShe passed the test easily
Irregularsgood → well · fast → fast · hard → hardShe speaks well · He runs fast
Position 1: before the verbsubject + adverb + verbShe quickly finished the report
Position 2: end of the sentence…verb + object + adverbShe finished the report quickly
Comparative adverbsfaster than · more carefully than · better / worseShe works more carefully than me
Traphard = duro · hardly = casi nadaHe works hard ≠ He hardly works
  • -ly quick → quickly — rápido → rápidamente
  • -ly slow → slowly — lento → lentamente
  • -ly careful → carefully — cuidadoso → cuidadosamente
  • -ly loud → loudly — fuerte → fuertemente (volumen)
  • -ly bad → badly — malo → mal
  • -ly dangerous → dangerously — peligroso → peligrosamente
  • -ily easy → easily — fácil → fácilmente
  • -ily happy → happily — feliz → felizmente
  • irreg good → well — bueno → bien
  • = fast → fast — rápido (no cambia)
  • = hard → hard — duro / con esfuerzo (no cambia)
  • = early → early — temprano (no cambia)

Practice

  1. She sings ___. (beautifully, beautiful, beauty)
  2. Kenji speaks Spanish very ___. (good, well, goodly)
  3. He works ___ than his brother. (carefullier, most carefully, more carefully)
  4. 'He hardly works' means ___. (he works very little, he works very hard, he works with his hands)
  5. Adverbs can go before the verb or at the end of the sentence: 'She quickly finished' / 'She finished quickly'.
  6. The adverb of 'good' is 'goodly'.
  7. Drive ___ (careful) — it's raining.
  8. She dances ___ (happy) at every party.
  9. He runs ___ (fast) than me.
  10. Unscramble: she, quickly, finished, the, report
  11. Unscramble: Ben, sings, loudly, in, the, shower
  12. Find the mistake: She sings beautiful.
  13. Find the mistake: He speaks English very good.
  14. Find the mistake: They work hardly every day.
  15. She finished the report quickly. — Move the adverb before the verb.
  16. He drives carefully. — Write the negative form.
  17. Do you sing well? — Answer with a short answer (No).
  18. She works quickly. — Write the opposite (use 'slowly').
  19. Ben dances badly. — Write the opposite (use 'well').
  20. Do you speak English well? (short answer)
  21. Did you wake up early today? (short answer, No)

Listening

Sofía: Team meeting! Kenji, you learned Spanish quickly. What's the secret?

Kenji: I practice daily, I listen carefully, and I make mistakes happily.

Ben: Then I'm doing great — I make mistakes more frequently than anyone!

Abuela Carmen: Making mistakes is fine, Ben. But you cut the onions dangerously. Slowly, my dear. Slowly.

Sofía: Meeting conclusion: practice daily, listen carefully, and cut onions slowly.

Reading

How do you learn a language well? Kenji wrote his method on one page. 'Listen carefully — more carefully than you speak. Practice daily, even badly: five bad minutes beat zero perfect ones. Speak bravely; people correct you kindly more often than you expect. Write slowly at first, then faster every week. And most importantly: compare yourself patiently — you will not improve as quickly as you want, but you will improve more steadily than you think.' Abuela Carmen read it and nodded wisely: 'The boy writes well.'

  • Kenji says you should listen more carefully than you speak.
  • He recommends practicing only when you can do it perfectly.
  • He says people usually correct you kindly.
  • Abuela Carmen thinks Kenji writes badly.
