Mixed Conditionals

B2 — grammar, writing, speaking

Kenji: If I hadn't applied for the exchange program, I wouldn't be sitting here right now.

Sofía: And if I hadn't studied design, I wouldn't have a studio to invite you to.

Ben: And if I hadn't lost my phone yesterday, I wouldn't be borrowing yours right now.

Sofía: Ben, that one's just today's problem, not a life philosophy.

Common mistake: “If she didn't study design, she wouldn't have run her studio now.” — Correct: “If she hadn't studied design, she wouldn't be running her studio now.”

Mixed conditionals combine a past condition with a present result (or the reverse) — a past action still affects how things are right now.

  • If Sofía hadn't studied design, she wouldn't be running her own studio now.
  • If Abuela Carmen hadn't sold mangoes as a girl, she wouldn't be so proud of her garden today.
  • If Ben had bought that waterproof case, he would still have his phone.
  • If Kenji hadn't decided to study abroad, he wouldn't be in Colombia right now.
  • If she hadn't moved to Medellín, she wouldn't know Ben and Kenji now.
  • If Ben hadn't lost his phone again, he wouldn't be borrowing mine right now.
  • If they hadn't missed the flight, they wouldn't still be at the airport.
  • Would you be here now if you hadn't taken that job?
  • If you hadn't studied English, would you understand this?
  • Would Sofía have her own studio if she hadn't studied design?
  • Would Sofía be a designer now if she hadn't studied it? No, she wouldn't.
  • Would Ben still have his phone if he'd bought a case? Yes, he would.
  • Is Kenji in Colombia because he studied abroad? Yes, he is.

Practice

  1. If Sofía ___ studied design, she wouldn't be running her studio now. (hadn't, didn't, hasn't)
  2. If Kenji hadn't applied, he ___ be in Colombia now. (wouldn't, won't, didn't)
  3. Mixed conditionals combine a past condition with a ___ result. (present, future, past)
  4. Mixed conditionals always use the same tense in both clauses.
  5. 'If she hadn't studied design, she wouldn't be a designer now' mixes past and present.
  6. The if-clause in this type uses the past perfect.
  7. If Abuela Carmen ___ (not/sell) mangoes as a girl, she wouldn't be so proud today.
  8. If Ben ___ (buy) that case, he would still have his phone.
  9. If they ___ (not/miss) the flight, they wouldn't still be at the airport.
  10. Unscramble: if, she, hadn't, studied, design, she, wouldn't, be, a, designer, now
  11. Unscramble: if, Kenji, hadn't, applied, he, wouldn't, be, here
  12. Find the mistake: If she didn't study design, she wouldn't have run her studio now.
  13. Sofía studied design. That's why she has a studio now. — Combine into a mixed conditional.
  14. Kenji applied for the exchange. That's why he's in Colombia now. — Combine into a mixed conditional.
  15. If Ben had bought the case, he would still have his phone. — Write the real situation as two simple statements (he DIDN'T buy it).
  16. If you had started English years ago, would you understand this now? (short answer, Yes)
  17. If Sofía hadn't studied design, would she have her own studio? (short answer, No)

Listening

Kenji: Sometimes I think about how different my life would be if I hadn't applied for this exchange.

Sofía: If you hadn't applied, you wouldn't be sitting in my studio right now, complaining about the rain.

Kenji: True. And if you hadn't studied design, none of us would have anywhere nice to sit.

Ben: And if I hadn't lost my phone, I wouldn't be so bored right now.

Sofía: Ben, that's not a mixed conditional, that's just Tuesday.

Reading

Abuela Carmen likes to remind her grandchildren that small past decisions shape a whole present. 'If I hadn't sold mangoes on that corner as a girl,' she tells Sofía, 'I wouldn't know how to run a business today — and you wouldn't have learned it from me.' Sofía agrees: if her grandmother hadn't taught her to negotiate prices at twelve years old, she probably wouldn't be as confident with clients now. Even Kenji has his own version: if he hadn't spent a summer failing at every conversation in Spanish class, he wouldn't be nearly fluent today. Some presents only make sense once you look back at the past that built them.

  • Abuela Carmen sold mangoes as a girl.
  • Sofía learned to negotiate prices from her grandmother.
  • Kenji found Spanish easy from the very beginning.
  • The text says presents have nothing to do with the past.
