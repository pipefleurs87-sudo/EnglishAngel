Emphatic Structures — do/does/did & so/such

C1 — grammar, speaking, writing

Kenji: You never seem to care about the results.

Sofía: I DO care, Kenji. I just don't panic out loud like you do.

Ben: It was such a stressful week that I actually finished something early, for once.

Abuela Carmen: The pressure DID do something good, then. Write that down before it disappears.

Common mistake: “I really understand why you're upset.” — Correct: “I DO understand why you're upset.”

Adding do/does/did before the base verb, or using so/such before an adjective or noun, adds emphasis — it insists on something that might be doubted.

Two ways to add emphasis
ToolStructureExample
do/does/did + base verbstressed auxiliary + verbI DO understand you.
so + adjective (+ that)so + adj + that + resultIt was so difficult that everyone struggled.
such (a/an) + adjective + noun (+ that)such + noun phrase + that + resultIt was such a good day that nobody left.
  • I DO understand why you're upset.
  • She DOES care about this project, even if it doesn't look like it.
  • It was such a good day that nobody wanted to leave.
  • The exam was so difficult that half the class failed.
  • It wasn't such a bad idea after all.
  • The traffic wasn't so bad that we missed the flight.
  • It didn't seem such a big deal at the time.
  • Do you really understand, or are you just saying that?
  • Was it such a good day that you didn't want to leave?
  • Did the exam really matter that much to you?
  • Do you actually understand? I DO understand, yes.
  • Was the exam that hard? Yes, it was so hard that half the class failed.
  • Did you enjoy the day? I DID enjoy it, actually.

Practice

  1. I ___ understand why you're upset. (DO, am, have)
  2. It was ___ good day that nobody wanted to leave. (such a, so, very)
  3. The exam was ___ difficult that half the class failed. (so, such, such a)
  4. 'I DO understand' is more emphatic than 'I understand'.
  5. 'So' is used directly before a noun (so a good day).
  6. 'Such' is used before an adjective + noun combination (such a good day).
  7. She ___ care about this project, even if it doesn't show.
  8. The traffic wasn't ___ bad that we missed the flight.
  9. It wasn't ___ bad idea after all.
  10. Unscramble: I, DO, understand, why, you're, upset
  11. Unscramble: it, was, such, a, good, day, that, nobody, left
  12. Find the mistake: It was so good day that nobody wanted to leave.
  13. I really do understand you. — Rewrite with emphatic stress using DO.
  14. The exam was very difficult; half the class failed. — Combine using 'so...that'.
  15. It was a very good day; nobody wanted to leave. — Combine using 'such a...that'.
  16. It was such a bad idea. — Write a version meaning the opposite (it wasn't bad).
  17. Do you really enjoy studying grammar? (answer with emphasis using DO)

Listening

Kenji: You never seem to care about the results.

Sofía: I DO care, Kenji. I just don't panic out loud like you do.

Ben: It was such a stressful week that I actually finished something early, for once.

Kenji: That IS surprising, coming from you.

Sofía: The pressure did do something good, then.

Ben: Don't get used to it. It was such a rare event.

Reading

Kenji has always assumed Sofía doesn't worry about deadlines — she never raises her voice, never seems rattled. 'I DO worry,' she told him once, 'I just don't show it the way you do.' It was such a strange thing to hear that Kenji actually stopped and reconsidered everything he thought he knew about her. The week that followed was so demanding that even Sofía's calm cracked a little — she DID admit, finally, that it had been hard. Ben, of all people, finished his part early that week, which was such a rare event that Abuela Carmen wrote the date down, just so nobody would forget it happened.

  • Sofía told Kenji she doesn't worry about deadlines.
  • Kenji reconsidered what he thought about Sofía.
  • Sofía admitted the week had been hard.
  • Ben finishing early was a common occurrence.
