Idioms — Time & Effort (Abuela Carmen's Wisdom)

B2 — vocabulary, reading, speaking, listening

Abuela Carmen: You look tired, Kenji.

Kenji: I burned the midnight oil studying for tomorrow's exam.

Abuela Carmen: Ben, on the other hand, will finish everything against the clock, in the nick of time, same as always.

Ben: I prefer to think of it as efficient. Time flies either way.

Common mistake: “I only see a blue moon once in a blue moon.” — Correct: “I only visit my hometown once in a blue moon.”

Idioms about time and effort describe urgency, rarity or hard work using images that have nothing literally to do with clocks or moons — the meaning is entirely figurative.

Time idioms — literal image vs. real meaning

Listening

Abuela Carmen: You look tired, Kenji.

Kenji: I burned the midnight oil studying for tomorrow's exam.

Abuela Carmen: Ben, on the other hand, will finish everything against the clock, in the nick of time, same as always.

Ben: I prefer to think of it as efficient.

Sofía: Efficient is building something from scratch the night before?

Ben: Time flies either way, doesn't it?

Reading

Abuela Carmen has a theory about time: some people, like Kenji, burn the midnight oil preparing for everything days in advance, just in case. Others, like Ben, seem to thrive working against the clock, somehow finishing everything in the nick of time and never a minute later. 'I built my whole business from scratch,' she tells them both, 'and I learned this: it doesn't matter which kind of person you are, as long as you finish.' She only gives this speech once in a blue moon — mostly, she says, because nobody needs to hear it twice. Time flies regardless of how you use it, she adds. Better to use it your own way.

  • Kenji prepares for things well in advance.
  • Ben usually finishes things days early.
  • Abuela Carmen built her business from scratch.
  • Abuela Carmen gives this speech every single day.
