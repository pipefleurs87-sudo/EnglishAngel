Used to — Past Habits That Are Gone

B1 — grammar, listening, reading, writing

Abuela Carmen: I used to live in the countryside. We used to swim in the river and dance cumbia every Saturday.

Kenji: Did you use to have electricity?

Abuela Carmen: Electricity? We used to have stars, my dear.

Ben: I used to have long hair. — Sofía: And we used to think it was a good idea. We don't anymore.

Common mistake: “I use to play soccer when I was a kid.” — Correct: “I used to play soccer when I was a kid.”

'Used to' describes habits and situations that were true in the past but are not true anymore — I used to..., but now I don't.

Listening

Kenji: Abuela, what did you use to do on Saturdays?

Abuela Carmen: We used to go to the river in the morning and dance cumbia at night.

Sofía: Did you use to cook with your mother?

Abuela Carmen: Every day. We didn't use to buy bread — we used to make it.

Ben: I used to make bread too! Once. It was... technically bread.

Abuela Carmen: My dear, some people used to call that a brick.

Reading

Medellín used to be a very different city. Abuela Carmen remembers: 'The metro didn't use to exist — we used to take old buses that broke down every week. People used to carry umbrellas every day, because you never knew. And on Sundays, the whole neighborhood used to eat together in the street.' Some things have changed: now there is a metro, and the buses are new. But some things haven't: 'We still eat together on Sundays,' she says. 'Some habits are too delicious to lose.'

  • The metro used to exist when Abuela Carmen was young.
  • The old buses used to break down every week.
  • The neighborhood used to eat together on Sundays.
  • The Sunday tradition has disappeared.
